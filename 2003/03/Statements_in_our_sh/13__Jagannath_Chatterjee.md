Dear Ravi,

Everyone on this list is right on this particular
issue. All arguements are valid. We must however note
that the Rishis were strict because the common masses
have a tendency to obey authority only. Most of what
has been prescribed is meant to be a deterent and
little else. If there were no fear of hell and no fear
of torture a large portion of the population would
have done as they pleased. What seems to us absurd
today has done its worth admirably. Even today most
turn to religion at an advanced age when death
threatens.

With the increase in individual freedom and
intelligence, the rapid strides of literacy, better
communication, such deterents may not now be
necessary. And seeing the Kaliyuga approach many
restrictions were also lifted so that religion becomes
a quest and not a burden.

I have always tried to have faith and avoided too much
arguements. It doesn't help. Common sense and
conscience are the best guides we can have. I would
rather root for blind faith than go in for learned
ignorance. Why dilly dally when we have so little
time? Charaibeti, charaibeti..... move on.

Regards
Jagannath

Namastubhyam,
Pranam to all. About a week back there was some discussion about learning Sanskrit. I believe it was Jaldhar Vyas who astutely pointed out that until one has learned the language one should be careful to rely too much on any one translation (he recommended using several translations of the same text simultaneously to pick up the nuances). This is very good advice indeed. Another member of our list offer gave sound advice to "listen" to Sanskrit as a means of learning, especially if one does not have a Sanskrit teacher nearby.
With that in mind, I offer the following:
1) http://www.carnatic.com/god/bhaja_govindam.htm
This is a complete transliteration and translation of Shankara's Bhaja Govindam. The opening paragraph (i.e., talk by Rajaji) and select verses are available on CD along with "Vishnu Sahasranama" by M.S. Subhalaxshmi.
2) Bhagavadgita
There are numerous versions of Gita recitations available on audio. Many are in traditional Vedic chanting rhythm, some even with commentary in Hindi. My personal favorite is a 2-volume set by Vanraj Bhatia (Living Media India Ltd). It's not too difficult to find in most Indian music stores. It takes approximately 100 verses from the Gita, and puts them to music, as if it were a theatrical performance. Admittedly, some do find this annoying, as it is not in the traditional Vedic rhythm. However, I personally enjoy it because the dramatic overtones are consistent (in my onion) the the content of the respective verses. The rhythm and intensity change with the theme being discussed between Arjuna and Sri Krishna. After all, were not the epics "plays" to be performed and and viewed?

Often when I sit down to memorize verses, and the vocabulary contained therein, I forget them the next day. However, after countless hours of listening to the Gita, even in the background as I'm doing housework, I have been able to remember many of them with much ease.

Anyway, my intent is not to give a sales pitch on this. I understand that everyone has his/her individual preferences. I just wanted to present these to the group for those interested in learning Sanskrit who may find the task a bit daunting.

Jai Sri Krishna
